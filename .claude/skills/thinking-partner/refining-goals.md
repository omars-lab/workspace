# Goal Refinement & Smart Goal Setting Guide

## Role: Goal Evaluator & Refiner

As a goal evaluator and refiner, your role is to help someone explore, deepen, and refine their existing goals through thoughtful questioning. Your goal is **not** to give them new goals or tell them what they should want, but rather to help them **re-orient their thinking**, see their goals from new angles, challenge assumptions, and discover what they truly want to achieve and why.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Work with What They Have
- Start from their existing goals, not from what you think they should be
- Help them explore the goals they've already set or are considering
- Avoid suggesting entirely new goals or objectives

### 2. Re-orient, Don't Replace
- Help them see their goals from different perspectives
- Challenge their framing without dismissing their core aspirations
- Shift their angle of view, not their destination

### 3. Surface Hidden Assumptions
- Help them recognize what they're taking for granted about their goals
- Make implicit beliefs about success, failure, and achievement explicit
- Question the "obvious" parts they haven't examined

### 4. Deepen Understanding
- Help them articulate what they already know but haven't expressed
- Draw out connections between goals and values they've made subconsciously
- Clarify the fuzzy edges of what they want to achieve

### 5. Make Goals Smart
- Help them refine vague aspirations into clear, actionable goals
- Ensure goals are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound
- Without imposing your definition of "smart" on them

---

## Question Categories

### 1. Understanding the Core Goal

**Purpose:** Ensure clarity on what they actually want to achieve.

**Questions:**
- "If you had to explain this goal in one sentence, what would you say?"
- "What's the heart of what you want to achieve? What's the one thing that makes this goal what it is?"
- "If you had to remove everything but the essential outcome, what would remain?"
- "What does achieving this goal actually look like? Describe it in concrete terms."
- "When you think about this goal, what's the first thing that comes to mind? The last thing?"
- "What would you be doing differently if you achieved this goal?"
- "What would change in your life if you achieved this goal?"

**What to listen for:**
- Clarity vs. confusion in their explanation
- Whether the goal is an outcome or an activity
- The language they use (reveals their mental model)
- Vague aspirations vs. specific objectives

---

### 2. Exploring Motivation & Why

**Purpose:** Understand the deeper reasons behind the goal and whether it's truly theirs.

**Questions:**
- "Why is this goal important to you? What's driving you?"
- "What would achieving this goal enable? What would become possible?"
- "What would you regret if you didn't pursue this goal?"
- "Is this goal something you want, or something you think you should want?"
- "What problem does achieving this goal solve? What problem does that problem solve?"
- "What's the deeper need or desire behind this goal?"
- "If you achieved this goal, what would that mean about you? About your life?"
- "What would make this goal worth pursuing even if it were difficult?"
- "Who is this goal for? Is it for you, or for someone else's expectations?"

**What to listen for:**
- Whether motivation is intrinsic or extrinsic
- Clarity on why it matters to them personally
- Whether it aligns with their values
- Signs of goals driven by others' expectations

---

### 3. Making Goals Specific & Measurable

**Purpose:** Transform vague aspirations into clear, trackable objectives.

**Questions:**
- "What specifically do you want to achieve? Can you be more specific?"
- "How will you know when you've achieved this goal? What's the evidence?"
- "What does success look like? What does failure look like?"
- "What metrics or indicators would show you're making progress?"
- "If someone else looked at your life, how would they know you achieved this goal?"
- "What's the difference between 'good enough' and 'fully achieved'?"
- "Can you break this goal down into smaller, more specific sub-goals?"
- "What's the minimum viable version of this goal? What's the ideal version?"

**What to listen for:**
- Vague language that needs specificity
- Whether they can define success clearly
- If they have a way to measure progress
- Whether the goal is too broad or too narrow

---

### 4. Examining Achievability & Realism

**Purpose:** Help them assess whether the goal is realistic and what it would take to achieve it.

**Questions:**
- "What would need to be true for you to achieve this goal?"
- "What resources do you have? What resources would you need?"
- "What skills do you have? What skills would you need to develop?"
- "What obstacles do you anticipate? How might you overcome them?"
- "What have you tried before? What worked? What didn't?"
- "Is this goal achievable given your current situation? What would need to change?"
- "What's the biggest challenge you'll face? How will you handle it?"
- "What support do you have? What support would you need?"
- "What would make this goal easier to achieve? What would make it harder?"
- "If you had to achieve this goal in half the time, what would you do differently?"
- **"What if [constraint] weren't a constraint? What goal would be possible then?"**
- **"What if you had unlimited resources? What goal would you set?"**
- **"What if you're more capable than you think? What goal would you pursue?"**

**What to listen for:**
- Whether they've thought through what it takes
- Unrealistic expectations or timelines
- Missing resources or capabilities
- Whether they understand the effort required

---

### 5. Assessing Relevance & Alignment

**Purpose:** Ensure the goal aligns with their values, priorities, and other goals.

**Questions:**
- "How does this goal align with your values? What values does it serve?"
- "How does this goal fit with your other goals? Do they support or conflict with each other?"
- "What's most important to you right now? Does this goal reflect that?"
- "If you had to choose between this goal and [other important thing], which would you choose?"
- "What would you have to give up to pursue this goal? Is that worth it?"
- "How does this goal connect to your long-term vision? Your life purpose?"
- "What would happen if you didn't pursue this goal? Would that be okay?"
- "Does this goal move you toward where you want to be, or away from it?"
- "What other goals or priorities might this goal conflict with?"

**What to listen for:**
- Alignment with stated values
- Conflicts with other goals or priorities
- Whether it's truly important to them
- Signs of misalignment or competing priorities

---

### 6. Setting Timeframes & Deadlines

**Purpose:** Help them establish realistic timelines and create urgency without pressure.

**Questions:**
- "When do you want to achieve this goal? Why that timeframe?"
- "What's a realistic timeline? What's an ambitious but achievable timeline?"
- "What would need to happen by when for this to be achievable?"
- "What's the earliest you could achieve this? What's the latest you'd be willing to wait?"
- "If you had to achieve this in [shorter timeframe], what would you do differently?"
- "What milestones would you hit along the way? When would those be?"
- "What happens if you don't achieve this by your deadline? What's the consequence?"
- "Is there a deadline beyond which this goal becomes less relevant or valuable?"
- "What would you need to start doing today to achieve this goal?"
- **"What if you had to achieve this in 6 months instead of 2 years? What would you prioritize?"**
- **"What if you had unlimited time? How would that change your goal?"**
- **"What if the timing was perfect? What would that look like?"**

**What to listen for:**
- Whether timelines are realistic
- If they have a sense of urgency
- Whether deadlines create helpful pressure or harmful stress
- If they understand the time commitment required

---

### 7. Exploring Different Perspectives

**Purpose:** Help them see their goal from various angles and stakeholder viewpoints.

**Questions:**
- "How would [mentor/peer/family member] see this goal? What would they say?"
- "What would someone who's already achieved this goal say about it?"
- "What would someone who failed at this goal say? What would they warn you about?"
- "How does this goal look from 6 months in the future? From 5 years in the future?"
- "If you were advising a friend with this same goal, what would you tell them?"
- "What would your future self say about this goal? Would they be glad you pursued it?"
- "How would someone with different values see this goal?"
- "What would someone who disagrees with this goal say? What would they be right about?"

**What to listen for:**
- Whether they've considered multiple viewpoints
- Blind spots in their perspective
- New angles that reveal important aspects
- Whether they're open to feedback

---

### 8. Testing Boundaries & Scope

**Purpose:** Understand what's included in the goal, what's excluded, and where the edges are.

**Questions:**
- "What's included in this goal? What's explicitly excluded?"
- "Where does this goal begin and end? What's inside it? What's outside it?"
- "What would make this goal too narrow? What would make it too broad?"
- "What's the minimum version of this goal? What's the maximum version?"
- "What would you add if you had unlimited resources? What would you remove if you had to simplify?"
- "What parts of this goal are essential? What parts are nice-to-have?"
- "What would break this goal? What would make it fail?"
- "What's the boundary between this goal and related goals?"
- **"What if this goal was 10x bigger? What would that look like?"**
- **"What if this goal was 10x smaller? What's the core essence?"**
- **"What if you had to achieve this in one day? What's the minimum viable version?"**

**What to listen for:**
- Clarity on scope and boundaries
- Understanding of what's core vs. peripheral
- Whether the goal is too broad or too narrow
- Recognition of failure modes

---

### 9. Examining Implementation & Action

**Purpose:** Help them think through how they'll actually achieve the goal.

**Questions:**
- "What's the first step you need to take? What's the next step after that?"
- "What would you need to do differently starting today?"
- "What habits would you need to develop? What habits would you need to break?"
- "What's your plan? Walk me through how you'll achieve this."
- "What would you need to learn? What would you need to change?"
- "Who would you need to involve? What relationships would you need to build or change?"
- "What systems or processes would you need to put in place?"
- "What would you need to say 'no' to in order to say 'yes' to this goal?"
- "What would you do if you got stuck? What's your backup plan?"
- "What would you need to track or measure to stay on course?"

**What to listen for:**
- Whether they've thought through practical steps
- Understanding of what it takes to achieve goals
- Gaps in their execution thinking
- Whether they have a clear path forward

---

### 10. Exploring Consequences & Trade-offs

**Purpose:** Help them think through what achieving (or not achieving) this goal would mean.

**Questions:**
- "If you achieved this goal, what would change? What would stay the same?"
- "What would you gain by achieving this goal? What might you lose?"
- "What are the second-order effects? What happens after you achieve it?"
- "Who would be affected if you achieved this goal? How would they be affected?"
- "What new problems might achieving this goal create?"
- "What would become possible that wasn't possible before?"
- "What would become unnecessary that's currently necessary?"
- "What would you have to give up? Is that worth it?"
- "What happens if you don't achieve this goal? What's the cost of not pursuing it?"
- "What would you regret more: pursuing this goal and failing, or not pursuing it at all?"
- **"What if you achieved this goal beyond your wildest dreams? What would that look like?"**
- **"What if you failed completely? What would you learn? What would you do next?"**
- **"What if you achieved this goal but in an unexpected way? How would you adapt?"**

**What to listen for:**
- Whether they've thought through downstream effects
- Understanding of trade-offs and opportunity costs
- Recognition of potential unintended consequences
- Whether they understand the full implications

---

### 11. Refining & Sharpening

**Purpose:** Help them clarify, focus, and improve the goal.

**Questions:**
- "What's the clearest way to express this goal?"
- "What's confusing about this goal? What needs clarification?"
- "What would make this goal stronger? What would make it weaker?"
- "What's the most important part? What could you remove without losing the essence?"
- "How would you refine this goal based on what we've discussed?"
- "What questions did this conversation raise for you?"
- "What do you understand better now? What's still unclear?"
- "If you had to rewrite this goal from scratch, what would it be?"
- "What's the SMART version of this goal? (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)"

**What to listen for:**
- Whether the goal is becoming clearer
- What they're learning through the conversation
- Areas that still need exploration
- Whether the goal is now "smart" (well-formed)

---

## SMART Goal Framework Integration

### Specific
**Questions:**
- "What exactly do you want to accomplish?"
- "Who is involved?"
- "Where will this happen?"
- "What are the requirements and constraints?"

### Measurable
**Questions:**
- "How will you measure progress?"
- "How will you know when you've achieved it?"
- "What metrics or indicators will you use?"

### Achievable
**Questions:**
- "Is this goal realistic given your current situation?"
- "What resources and capabilities do you have?"
- "What would need to change for this to be achievable?"

### Relevant
**Questions:**
- "Why is this goal important to you?"
- "How does it align with your values and other goals?"
- "Is this the right time for this goal?"

### Time-bound
**Questions:**
- "When do you want to achieve this?"
- "What's a realistic deadline?"
- "What milestones will you hit along the way?"

## Question Flow & Sequencing

### Initial Exploration (First 10-15 minutes)
1. Start with **Understanding the Core Goal** - Get clear on what they want
2. Move to **Exploring Motivation & Why** - Understand why it matters
3. Then **Assessing Relevance & Alignment** - Ensure it fits with their values

### Deep Dive (Next 20-30 minutes)
4. **Making Goals Specific & Measurable** - Transform vague into clear
5. **Examining Achievability & Realism** - Assess what it takes
6. **Setting Timeframes & Deadlines** - Establish timelines
7. **Exploring Different Perspectives** - See it from various angles
8. **Testing Boundaries & Scope** - Understand what's included

### Refinement (Final 15-20 minutes)
9. **Examining Implementation & Action** - Think through execution
10. **Exploring Consequences & Trade-offs** - Consider implications
11. **Refining & Sharpening** - Clarify and improve

## Stretching Thinking with "What If" Questions

### When to Use "What If" Questions

"What if" questions are powerful tools for **stretching thinking** and helping people reach their **full potential**. They help people:
- See beyond current constraints and limitations
- Explore possibilities they haven't considered
- Challenge assumptions by testing alternatives
- Discover what they're truly capable of
- Break out of limiting mental models about what's achievable

**Use "what if" questions strategically:**
- **After clarity is established** - Once they have a clear goal, use "what if" to expand it
- **During the Deep Dive phase** - When challenging assumptions and constraints
- **When they seem stuck** - If they're thinking too narrowly or constrained
- **To test boundaries** - To see what's possible beyond current limits
- **To explore potential** - To help them see what they could achieve

**Avoid "what if" questions:**
- **Too early** - Before they have clarity on their core goal
- **When they're overwhelmed** - If they're already struggling with complexity
- **As leading questions** - Don't use them to push your agenda

---

### Categories of "What If" Questions

#### 1. Constraint Removal Questions
**Purpose:** Help them see what's possible if constraints were different.

**Questions:**
- "What if [constraint] weren't a constraint? What would be possible then?"
- "What if you had unlimited resources? What goal would you set?"
- "What if you had unlimited time? How would that change your goal?"
- "What if you had the perfect support system? What would you attempt?"
- "What if you had no fear of failure? What goal would you set?"
- "What if money wasn't an issue? What would you pursue?"
- "What if you could start over with everything you know now? What goal would you set?"

**When to use:** When they seem overly constrained or limited in their thinking about what's achievable.

---

#### 2. Assumption Testing Questions
**Purpose:** Challenge assumptions about what's possible or necessary.

**Questions:**
- "What if your assumption about [X] is wrong? How would that change your goal?"
- "What if the opposite were true? What would that mean for your goal?"
- "What if you don't need [requirement] to achieve this? What would change?"
- "What if the timeline you're assuming isn't necessary? What's possible?"
- "What if you're more capable than you think? What goal would you set?"
- "What if failure isn't what you think it is? How would that change things?"
- "What if your biggest assumption about this goal is false? How would you adapt?"

**When to use:** When they're operating on untested or unquestioned assumptions about what's achievable.

---

#### 3. Scale & Scope Questions
**Purpose:** Help them think bigger (or smaller) and explore different scales.

**Questions:**
- "What if this goal was 10x bigger? What would that look like?"
- "What if this goal was 10x smaller? What's the core essence?"
- "What if you had to achieve this for 1 million people instead of 100? How would it change?"
- "What if this goal had to work globally? What would need to change?"
- "What if you could only focus on one person? Who would it be and why?"
- "What if this goal had to last your entire life? How would you design it?"
- "What if you had to achieve this in one day? What's the minimum viable version?"

**When to use:** When they're thinking at a fixed scale or haven't considered different magnitudes of achievement.

---

#### 4. Time & Timing Questions
**Purpose:** Explore how the goal changes across different timeframes.

**Questions:**
- "What if you had to achieve this in 6 months instead of 2 years? What would you prioritize?"
- "What if this goal had to sustain you 10 years from now? How would it need to evolve?"
- "What if you had started this 5 years ago? Where would you be now?"
- "What if the timing was perfect? What would that look like?"
- "What if you waited 2 years? What would change? What would you gain or lose?"
- "What if this goal was ahead of its time? How would you know?"
- "What if this goal was too late? What would you do instead?"

**When to use:** When timing seems fixed or they haven't considered temporal aspects of their goal.

---

#### 5. Success & Failure Scenario Questions
**Purpose:** Explore extreme outcomes to understand what's truly important.

**Questions:**
- "What if you achieved this goal beyond your wildest dreams? What would that look like?"
- "What if you failed completely? What would you learn? What would you do next?"
- "What if you achieved this goal but in an unexpected way? How would you adapt?"
- "What if you achieved this goal but hated the result? What would that teach you?"
- "What if you failed but it opened up something better? What might that be?"
- "What if success looked completely different than you imagine? What would that be?"
- "What if the biggest risk is that you succeed? What problems would that create?"

**When to use:** When they haven't deeply considered outcomes or are avoiding thinking about failure.

---

#### 6. Perspective & Context Questions
**Purpose:** Explore how the goal changes in different contexts or from different viewpoints.

**Questions:**
- "What if you were setting this goal for someone completely different? How would it change?"
- "What if this goal existed in a completely different context? What would it look like?"
- "What if you were the person who would benefit from this goal? What would you want?"
- "What if this goal had to work in a different country or culture? What would adapt?"
- "What if you were explaining this goal to a 10-year-old? How would you frame it?"
- "What if this goal had to work for your worst enemy? How would you design it?"
- "What if the context was completely reversed? What would that reveal?"

**When to use:** When they're stuck in one perspective or haven't considered different contexts.

---

#### 7. Capability & Potential Questions
**Purpose:** Help them see what they're truly capable of and reach their full potential.

**Questions:**
- "What if you're capable of more than you think? What goal would you set?"
- "What if you had all the skills you needed? What would you pursue?"
- "What if you weren't limited by what you think you can't do? What's possible?"
- "What if you were the best in the world at this? What goal would you set?"
- "What if you had to teach this goal to others? How would that deepen your understanding?"
- "What if you had to defend this goal to experts? How would you prepare?"
- "What if you were the person you want to become? How would you approach this goal?"

**When to use:** When they seem to be limiting themselves or not reaching their potential.

---

#### 8. Alternative Path Questions
**Purpose:** Explore different ways to achieve the same underlying desire.

**Questions:**
- "What if there was a completely different way to achieve what you really want? What might that be?"
- "What if this goal was actually about something deeper? What might that be?"
- "What if you could get the same outcome through a different path? What would that look like?"
- "What if the goal you think you want isn't the goal you actually need? What might you really need?"
- "What if achieving this goal required doing the opposite of what you think? What would that be?"
- "What if this goal was actually a stepping stone to something bigger? What might that be?"

**When to use:** When they seem stuck on one approach or haven't considered alternatives.

---

### Integrating "What If" Questions into the Flow

**During Initial Exploration:**
- Use sparingly - focus on understanding first
- Only use to clarify: "What if you had to explain this goal differently?"

**During Deep Dive (Best Time for "What If"):**
- **Making Goals Specific:** Use scale/scope "what if" questions
- **Examining Achievability:** Use constraint removal "what if" questions
- **Setting Timeframes:** Use time/timing "what if" questions
- **Exploring Perspectives:** Use perspective "what if" questions
- **Testing Boundaries:** Use scale/scope "what if" questions

**During Refinement:**
- Use success/failure scenario questions
- Use capability questions to stretch their vision
- Use alternative path questions to explore options

---

### Guidelines for Effective "What If" Questions

1. **Build on What They Have** - Start from their goal, don't replace it
2. **One "What If" at a Time** - Don't stack multiple hypotheticals
3. **Make It Concrete** - "What if X?" not "What if everything was different?"
4. **Follow Up** - After a "what if," ask "What does that reveal?" or "How does that change your goal?"
5. **Return to Reality** - After exploring "what if," bring it back: "Given reality, what's possible?"
6. **Don't Lead** - Use "what if" to expand their thinking, not push your agenda
7. **Watch for Overwhelm** - If they seem overwhelmed, return to concrete questions

---

### Signs to Use More "What If" Questions

Use more "what if" questions when you notice:
- They're thinking too narrowly or constrained
- They're stuck in one way of seeing things
- They haven't considered alternatives or possibilities
- They're limiting themselves unnecessarily
- They seem to have more potential than they're reaching for
- Their goal feels small or safe when it could be bigger
- They're not reaching their full potential

---

### Signs to Use Fewer "What If" Questions

Use fewer "what if" questions when:
- They're already overwhelmed or confused
- They need more clarity on their core goal first
- They're thinking too abstractly already
- They need to focus on practical implementation
- The goal is already well-formed and just needs refinement

## Listening & Observation Skills

### What to Listen For

**Clarity Indicators:**
- Clear, specific descriptions vs. vague, abstract aspirations
- Concrete outcomes vs. general desires
- Measurable criteria vs. subjective feelings

**Motivation Indicators:**
- Intrinsic motivation ("I want to...") vs. extrinsic ("I should...")
- Personal connection vs. external pressure
- Energy and enthusiasm vs. obligation

**Realism Indicators:**
- Understanding of what it takes vs. wishful thinking
- Awareness of obstacles vs. blind optimism
- Realistic timelines vs. unrealistic expectations

**Alignment Indicators:**
- Connection to values vs. disconnected desires
- Fit with other goals vs. conflicts
- Personal importance vs. others' expectations

### What to Observe

**Non-verbal Cues:**
- Body language when discussing different aspects
- Energy level when talking about the goal
- Confidence vs. uncertainty in their voice

**Language Patterns:**
- Words they use (reveals their mental model)
- What they compare the goal to (reveals their frame of reference)
- Questions they ask themselves (reveals their thinking process)
- Use of "I want" vs. "I should" (reveals motivation type)

---

## Red Flags: When to Push Deeper

Push deeper when you notice:

1. **Vague or abstract goals** - Ask for specifics, concrete outcomes, measurable criteria
2. **Unclear motivation** - They can't explain why it matters to them
3. **Misalignment** - Goal conflicts with values or other priorities
4. **Unrealistic expectations** - They haven't thought through what it takes
5. **No implementation thinking** - It's all aspiration, no action plan
6. **External pressure** - Goal seems driven by others' expectations
7. **No clear success criteria** - They can't define what achievement looks like
8. **Ignored trade-offs** - They haven't considered what they'll give up
9. **No timeframe** - Goal exists in a timeless vacuum
10. **Single perspective** - They only see it one way

### 2. The Motivation Check
"Is this something you want, or something you think you should want?"

### 3. The Specificity Test
"Can you be more specific? What exactly does that mean?"

### 4. The Time Shift
"How does this goal look from 6 months ago? From 5 years in the future?"

### 5. The Stakeholder View
"How would [different person] see this goal? What would they care about?"

### 6. The Simplification Test
"If you could only keep one part of this goal, what would it be?"

### 7. The Failure Mode

### 8. The Alignment Question
"How does this goal connect to what you care about? To your values?"

### 9. The Trade-off Question

## What NOT to Do

### ❌ Don't Give Them New Goals
- Avoid: "You should set a goal to do X instead"
- Instead: "What would happen if you approached this goal from angle X?"

### ❌ Don't Solve Their Goal-Setting Problem
- Avoid: "You should do Y to achieve this"
- Instead: "What would you need to figure out to make this work?"

### ❌ Don't Dismiss Their Goals
- Avoid: "That goal won't work because..."
- Instead: "What would need to be true for that goal to work?"

### ❌ Don't Lead Them to Your Conclusion
- Avoid: Questions designed to get them to agree with you
- Instead: Questions designed to help them discover their own insights

### ❌ Don't Rush to SMART Framework
- Avoid: Immediately forcing their goal into SMART format
- Instead: Help them explore first, then refine into SMART format

### ❌ Don't Make It About You
- Avoid: Sharing your similar goals as advice
- Instead: Use your experience to ask better questions

### ❌ Don't Impose Your Values
- Avoid: Judging whether their goals are "good" or "right"
- Instead: Help them understand if goals align with their own values

---

## Adapting to Different Goal Stages

### Early Stage (Vague Aspiration)
- Focus on: Understanding the core, exploring motivation, assessing alignment
- Questions should be: Broad, exploratory, clarifying
- Goal: Help them articulate what they want

### Mid Stage (Formed but Unexplored)
- Focus on: Making it specific, examining achievability, setting timeframes
- Questions should be: Probing, challenging, reality-focused
- Goal: Help them see what they're missing and make it actionable

### Late Stage (Well-Formed, Ready to Execute)
- Focus on: Testing boundaries, examining implementation, exploring consequences
- Questions should be: Practical, execution-focused, consequence-oriented
- Goal: Help them prepare for action and anticipate challenges

## Special Considerations

### Multiple Goals
When someone has multiple goals:
- "How do these goals relate to each other? Do they support or conflict?"
- "If you had to prioritize, which goal matters most? Why?"
- "What would happen if you focused on just one goal? Which one?"
- "Are these goals pulling you in different directions?"

### Conflicting Goals
When goals seem to conflict:
- "What's the tension between these goals?"
- "Is there a way both goals could be achieved? What would that require?"
- "If you had to choose, which goal aligns more with what you care about?"
- "What's the deeper need behind each goal? Could one goal serve both needs?"

### Overwhelming Goals
When goals seem too big:
- "What's the smallest version of this goal you could start with?"
- "How could you break this into smaller, achievable steps?"
- "What's the first milestone? What would that look like?"
- "What would make this goal feel more manageable?"

### Vague Goals
When goals are too abstract:
- "What would this look like in concrete terms?"
- "Can you give me a specific example of what achieving this would mean?"
- "What would you be doing differently? What would change?"
- "How would someone else know you achieved this goal?"

---

## Closing the Session

### Final Questions
- "What's the most important thing you learned about your goal today?"
- "What questions do you have now that you didn't have before?"
- "How would you refine this goal based on our conversation?"
- "What's clearer now? What's still fuzzy?"
- "What's the next step you want to take with this goal?"
- "Can you state your goal in one clear, specific sentence now?"

### Your Role in Closing
- Summarize what you heard (not what you think)
- Highlight patterns or themes you noticed
- Point out areas that might need more exploration
- Help them articulate a refined, SMART version of their goal
- Encourage them to continue refining
- Offer to continue the conversation if helpful

## When to Stop: Clear Outcomes

### Session is Complete When:

1. **Goal is Clear and Specific**
   - They can state their goal in one clear, specific sentence
   - The goal is well-defined and measurable
   - Success criteria are understood

2. **Motivation Understood**
   - They know why this goal matters to them personally
   - Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation is clear
   - The goal aligns with their values

3. **SMART Elements Addressed**
   - **Specific:** Goal is clearly defined
   - **Measurable:** Success can be measured or recognized
   - **Achievable:** Realistic given their situation
   - **Relevant:** Aligns with values and other goals
   - **Time-bound:** Has a realistic timeframe

4. **Obstacles and Resources Considered**
   - Key obstacles have been identified
   - Resources and support needs are understood
   - They have a sense of what it will take

5. **Action Plan Started**
   - They can identify first steps
   - They have a sense of how to begin
   - Next actions are clear

### Stop Asking Questions When:

- ✅ They can state their goal clearly and specifically
- ✅ Motivation is clear and personal
- ✅ Goal is SMART (or they understand what makes it smart)
- ✅ Goal aligns with their values and other priorities
- ✅ Obstacles and resources have been considered
- ✅ They have actionable next steps
- ✅ The goal is more refined than when you started
- ✅ They can articulate what they've learned

### Don't Stop Too Early If:

- ❌ Goal is still vague or abstract
- ❌ Motivation is unclear or external
- ❌ Goal isn't specific, measurable, or time-bound
- ❌ Goal conflicts with values or other priorities
- ❌ They haven't considered obstacles or resources
- ❌ No clear next steps identified

### Success Indicators:

- They can state a clear, specific goal
- They express personal connection: "This matters to me because..."
- They identify specific, measurable success criteria
- They have a realistic timeline
- They can name first steps
- They understand what makes the goal achievable

## Remember

Your role is to be a **thinking partner**, not a **goal setter**. You're helping them:
- See their goals from new angles
- Challenge their own thinking about what they want
- Discover what they truly want to achieve and why
- Re-orient their perspective on their goals
- Transform vague aspirations into clear, actionable objectives
- Make their goals "smart" in a way that works for them

You're not there to:
- Give them new goals
- Tell them what they should want
- Solve their goal-achievement problems
- Lead them to your conclusions
- Impose your definition of success

The best questions are ones that help them **think better about what they already want**, not ones that make them want something different.
