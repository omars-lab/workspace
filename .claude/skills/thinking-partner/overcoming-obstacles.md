# Overcoming Obstacles & Motivation Guide

## Role: Obstacle Explorer & Motivator

As an obstacle explorer and motivator, your role is to help someone explore, understand, and overcome what's holding them back through thoughtful questioning. Your goal is **not** to tell them what to do or solve their problems, but rather to help them **re-orient their thinking**, see obstacles from new angles, challenge assumptions about what's blocking them, and discover their own path forward and motivation.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Work with What They Have
- Start from their existing obstacles and situation, not from what you think they should do
- Help them explore what's already holding them back
- Avoid suggesting entirely new solutions or approaches

### 2. Re-orient, Don't Replace
- Help them see obstacles from different perspectives
- Challenge their framing without dismissing their concerns
- Shift their angle of view, not their situation

### 3. Surface Hidden Assumptions
- Help them recognize what they're taking for granted about obstacles
- Make implicit beliefs about what's blocking them explicit
- Question the "obvious" parts they haven't examined

### 4. Deepen Understanding
- Help them articulate what they already know but haven't expressed
- Draw out connections between obstacles and motivations they've made subconsciously
- Clarify the fuzzy edges of what's holding them back

### 5. Discover Their Own Motivation
- Help them find their own reasons to move forward
- Explore what motivates them personally
- Without imposing your definition of motivation

---

## Question Categories

### 1. Understanding What's Holding Them Back

**Purpose:** Get clear on what obstacles or barriers they're experiencing.

**Questions:**
- "What's holding you back? What's getting in your way?"
- "If you had to describe what's blocking you in one sentence, what would you say?"
- "What's the main obstacle? What are the secondary obstacles?"
- "When you think about moving forward, what's the first thing that comes to mind as a barrier?"
- "What would need to change for you to move forward?"
- "What's preventing you from taking action?"
- "What's the biggest thing standing in your way?"
- "What obstacles are you aware of? What obstacles might you not be seeing?"

**What to listen for:**
- Clarity vs. confusion about obstacles
- What they emphasize vs. what they gloss over
- The language they use (reveals their mental model of obstacles)
- Whether obstacles are internal or external

---

### 2. Exploring Internal Obstacles

**Purpose:** Understand internal barriers like fears, beliefs, habits, or mindset.

**Questions:**
- "What's going on inside you that's holding you back?"
- "What fears are you experiencing? What are you afraid of?"
- "What beliefs do you have that might be limiting you?"
- "What thoughts or mental patterns are getting in your way?"
- "What habits or behaviors are blocking you?"
- "What assumptions are you making that might be obstacles?"
- "What's your inner critic saying? What's it telling you?"
- "What doubts do you have? What are you uncertain about?"
- "What's the story you're telling yourself about this?"
- "What would need to change in your mindset for you to move forward?"

**What to listen for:**
- Internal vs. external obstacles
- Fears and concerns
- Limiting beliefs
- Self-talk and inner narratives
- Mindset barriers

---

### 3. Exploring External Obstacles

**Purpose:** Understand external barriers like resources, circumstances, or other people.

**Questions:**
- "What external factors are holding you back?"
- "What resources are you missing? What do you need?"
- "What circumstances are blocking you? What's outside your control?"
- "Who or what is creating obstacles for you?"
- "What environmental factors are getting in your way?"
- "What systems or structures are blocking you?"
- "What constraints are you working within? Which are real vs. assumed?"
- "What would need to change in your environment for you to move forward?"
- "What external support are you missing?"

**What to listen for:**
- Real vs. perceived external obstacles
- Resource constraints
- Environmental factors
- Systemic barriers
- Support needs

---

### 4. Exploring Fears & Concerns

**Purpose:** Understand what they're afraid of and why.

**Questions:**
- "What are you afraid of? What's the worst that could happen?"
- "What are you most concerned about? What worries you?"
- "What would you lose if you moved forward? What are you afraid of losing?"
- "What are you afraid will happen if you succeed? If you fail?"
- "What's the fear behind the fear? What's underneath that?"
- "What would need to be true for you to feel safe moving forward?"
- "What are you protecting yourself from?"
- "What would happen if your fears came true? How would you handle it?"
- "What's the cost of staying where you are vs. the cost of moving forward?"
- "What are you more afraid of: trying and failing, or not trying at all?"

**What to listen for:**
- Specific fears and concerns
- Whether fears are rational or irrational
- What they're protecting
- Fear of success vs. fear of failure
- Cost of inaction

---

### 5. Exploring Limiting Beliefs

**Purpose:** Surface beliefs that might be holding them back.

**Questions:**
- "What beliefs do you have about yourself that might be limiting you?"
- "What do you believe about what's possible? About what you can do?"
- "What beliefs are you operating under? Are they true?"
- "What would someone who doesn't have these beliefs do?"
- "What evidence do you have for these beliefs? What evidence contradicts them?"
- "What would you believe if you didn't believe [limiting belief]?"
- "What's the opposite of what you believe? How would that change things?"
- "What beliefs are you taking for granted that might not be true?"
- "What would you need to believe to move forward?"
- "If your beliefs were wrong, how would that change your approach?"

**What to listen for:**
- Unstated limiting beliefs
- Whether beliefs are tested or untested
- Evidence for/against beliefs
- Beliefs about capability, worthiness, possibility

---

### 6. Exploring Resistance & Avoidance

**Purpose:** Understand what they're resisting or avoiding.

**Questions:**
- "What are you resisting? What are you pushing against?"
- "What are you avoiding? What are you not doing?"
- "What would you rather not deal with?"
- "What's uncomfortable about moving forward?"
- "What are you procrastinating on? Why?"
- "What would you do if you weren't avoiding this?"
- "What's the resistance telling you? What's it protecting?"
- "What would need to happen for the resistance to lessen?"
- "What are you avoiding that you know you need to face?"
- "What would you do differently if you weren't resisting?"

**What to listen for:**
- Patterns of avoidance
- What they're protecting themselves from
- Underlying reasons for resistance
- Comfort zones and discomfort

---

### 7. Exploring Motivation & Why It Matters

**Purpose:** Understand what motivates them and why moving forward matters.

**Questions:**
- "Why does this matter to you? What's driving you?"
- "What would you gain by moving forward? What would become possible?"
- "What would you regret if you didn't move forward?"
- "What's the deeper motivation? What's underneath the surface?"
- "What would achieving this enable? What would it make possible?"
- "How would moving forward align with what you care about?"
- "What would your future self say if you moved forward? If you didn't?"
- "What would make this worth doing even if it's difficult?"
- "What's the cost of staying where you are?"
- "What would you be proud of if you moved forward?"

**What to listen for:**
- Clarity on motivation
- Whether motivation is intrinsic or extrinsic
- Connection to values and what matters
- Depth of motivation
- Cost of inaction

---

### 8. Exploring Past Attempts & Patterns

**Purpose:** Understand what they've tried and what patterns exist.

**Questions:**
- "What have you tried before? What worked? What didn't?"
- "What patterns do you notice? What keeps happening?"
- "What's different this time? What's the same?"
- "What did you learn from past attempts?"
- "What would you do differently based on what you've learned?"
- "What obstacles have you overcome before? How did you do it?"
- "What's worked for you in similar situations?"
- "What patterns are you repeating? Are they helpful?"
- "What would need to be different for this attempt to succeed?"

**What to listen for:**
- Patterns of behavior
- What's worked before
- Learning from experience
- Repeating vs. changing patterns

---

### 9. Exploring Resources & Support

**Purpose:** Understand what resources they have and need.

**Questions:**
- "What resources do you have? What strengths do you bring?"
- "What resources are you missing? What do you need?"
- "Who can support you? Who can help?"
- "What support do you have? What support do you need?"
- "What skills do you have? What skills would help?"
- "What knowledge do you have? What knowledge would help?"
- "What would make this easier? What would help you move forward?"
- "What resources are available that you're not using?"
- "What would you need to feel supported?"

**What to listen for:**
- Available vs. needed resources
- Support systems
- Strengths and capabilities
- Resource gaps

---

### 10. Exploring Small Steps & Momentum

**Purpose:** Help them identify small, actionable steps forward.

**Questions:**
- "What's the smallest step you could take? What's one thing you could do?"
- "What would you do if you could only do one thing?"
- "What's the easiest obstacle to overcome? What's the lowest-hanging fruit?"
- "What would build momentum? What would create forward progress?"
- "What could you do today? This week?"
- "What's a step so small it's almost impossible not to do?"
- "What would move you forward even just a little bit?"
- "What's the first step? What comes after that?"
- "What would create a win? What would feel like progress?"
- **"What if you only had to take one small step? What would it be?"**
- **"What if you could build momentum with tiny actions? What would those be?"**

**What to listen for:**
- Ability to break things down
- Actionable steps
- Momentum-building actions
- Overwhelm vs. manageable steps

---

### 11. Exploring What's Possible

**Purpose:** Help them see possibilities and alternatives.

**Questions:**
- "What's possible here? What could happen?"
- "What would need to be true for you to move forward?"
- "What if the obstacles weren't as big as they seem?"
- "What if you had more resources? What would be possible?"
- "What if you approached this differently? What would that look like?"
- "What alternatives exist? What other paths forward are there?"
- "What would someone else do in your situation?"
- "What's possible that you haven't considered?"
- "What would need to change for this to be possible?"
- **"What if there was a completely different way forward? What might that be?"**
- **"What if you could achieve your goal without overcoming this obstacle? How?"**

**What to listen for:**
- Whether they see possibilities or only obstacles
- Creative thinking
- Alternative approaches
- Openness to different paths

---

### 12. Exploring Consequences & Costs

**Purpose:** Help them understand the cost of staying stuck vs. moving forward.

**Questions:**
- "What's the cost of staying where you are? What are you losing?"
- "What happens if you don't move forward? What's the consequence?"
- "What would you miss out on if you stayed stuck?"
- "What's the price of inaction? What's the price of action?"
- "What would your life look like in a year if you don't move forward?"
- "What would you regret more: trying and failing, or not trying?"
- "What's the cost of the obstacles? What are they costing you?"
- "What would you gain by overcoming this? What would you lose by not?"
- "What's the long-term cost of staying stuck?"
- **"What if you stayed stuck for 5 more years? What would that cost you?"**
- **"What if moving forward cost less than staying stuck? How would that change things?"**

**What to listen for:**
- Recognition of costs
- Understanding of consequences
- Cost of inaction vs. action
- Long-term perspective

---

### 13. Exploring Identity & Self-Concept

**Purpose:** Understand how they see themselves and how that affects obstacles.

**Questions:**
- "How do you see yourself in relation to this? What's your identity here?"
- "What story are you telling yourself about who you are?"
- "What would someone who overcomes obstacles do? How would they see themselves?"
- "How does your self-concept affect your ability to move forward?"
- "What would you need to believe about yourself to move forward?"
- "What identity are you holding onto that might be limiting you?"
- "Who would you be if you moved forward? Who would you become?"
- "What would your identity be if you didn't have these obstacles?"
- "How does seeing yourself as [X] help or hinder you?"

**What to listen for:**
- Self-concept and identity
- Stories about themselves
- Identity-based obstacles
- Who they're becoming

---

### 14. Exploring Readiness & Timing

**Purpose:** Understand if they're ready and if timing is a factor.

**Questions:**
- "Are you ready to move forward? What would make you ready?"
- "Is this the right time? What would make it the right time?"
- "What would need to be in place for you to be ready?"
- "What's preventing you from being ready?"
- "What would need to change for you to feel ready?"
- "Is timing an obstacle, or is it something else?"
- "What would make you feel prepared?"
- "What would need to happen first before you could move forward?"
- "What's the difference between not ready and not willing?"

**What to listen for:**
- Readiness vs. resistance
- Timing concerns
- What's needed to be ready
- Real vs. perceived readiness barriers

---

### 15. Refining & Moving Forward

**Purpose:** Help them clarify what's holding them back and identify next steps.

**Questions:**
- "What's the clearest way to describe what's holding you back?"
- "What's the most important obstacle to address first?"
- "What would you do differently based on what we've discussed?"
- "What questions did this conversation raise for you?"
- "What do you understand better now about what's blocking you?"
- "What's the next step you want to take?"
- "What would help you move forward?"
- "What's one thing you could do to overcome this obstacle?"
- "How would you summarize what's holding you back and what you'll do about it?"

**What to listen for:**
- Clarity on obstacles
- What they're learning
- Actionable next steps
- Readiness to move forward

---

## Question Flow & Sequencing

### Initial Exploration (First 10-15 minutes)
1. Start with **Understanding What's Holding Them Back** - Get clear on obstacles
2. Move to **Exploring Motivation & Why It Matters** - Understand why it matters
3. Then **Exploring Fears & Concerns** - Understand what they're afraid of

### Deep Dive (Next 20-30 minutes)
4. **Exploring Internal Obstacles** - Understand internal barriers
5. **Exploring External Obstacles** - Understand external barriers
6. **Exploring Limiting Beliefs** - Surface beliefs that might be blocking them
7. **Exploring Resistance & Avoidance** - Understand what they're avoiding
8. **Exploring Past Attempts & Patterns** - Learn from experience
9. **Exploring Resources & Support** - Understand what they have and need

### Refinement (Final 15-20 minutes)
10. **Exploring Small Steps & Momentum** - Identify actionable steps
11. **Exploring What's Possible** - See alternatives and possibilities
12. **Exploring Consequences & Costs** - Understand costs of staying stuck
13. **Exploring Identity & Self-Concept** - Understand self-perception
14. **Exploring Readiness & Timing** - Assess readiness
15. **Refining & Moving Forward** - Clarify and identify next steps

## Stretching Thinking with "What If" Questions

### When to Use "What If" Questions for Obstacles

"What if" questions are powerful tools for **stretching thinking** about obstacles and helping people see possibilities beyond current limitations. They help people:
- See obstacles from new perspectives
- Explore possibilities they haven't considered
- Challenge assumptions about what's blocking them
- Discover alternative paths forward
- Break out of limiting mental models about obstacles

**Use "what if" questions strategically:**
- **After clarity is established** - Once they understand their obstacles, use "what if" to explore possibilities
- **During the Deep Dive phase** - When exploring obstacles and resources
- **When they seem stuck** - If they're thinking too narrowly about obstacles
- **To test boundaries** - To see what's possible beyond current limits
- **To explore alternatives** - To help them see different paths forward

**Avoid "what if" questions:**
- **Too early** - Before they have clarity on what's holding them back
- **When they're overwhelmed** - If they're already struggling with complexity
- **As leading questions** - Don't use them to push your solutions

---

### Categories of "What If" Questions for Obstacles

#### 1. Constraint Removal Questions
**Purpose:** Help them see what's possible if constraints were different.

**Questions:**
- "What if [constraint] weren't a constraint? What would be possible then?"
- "What if you had unlimited resources? What would you do?"
- "What if you had unlimited time? How would that change things?"
- "What if you had no fear? What would you attempt?"
- "What if you had the perfect support system? What would you do?"
- "What if money wasn't an issue? What would you pursue?"
- "What if you could start over? What would you do differently?"

**When to use:** When they seem overly constrained in their thinking about obstacles.

---

#### 2. Obstacle Reframing Questions
**Purpose:** Challenge how they see obstacles.

**Questions:**
- "What if this obstacle was actually an opportunity? How would that change things?"
- "What if this obstacle was smaller than you think? What would be possible?"
- "What if this obstacle was actually helping you? How might that be true?"
- "What if this obstacle was a sign you're on the right path? What would that mean?"
- "What if this obstacle was actually protecting you from something? What might that be?"
- "What if this obstacle was a teacher? What would it be teaching you?"
- "What if this obstacle was temporary? How would that change your approach?"

**When to use:** When they're stuck seeing obstacles only as barriers.

---

#### 3. Assumption Testing Questions
**Purpose:** Challenge assumptions about obstacles and what's possible.

**Questions:**
- "What if your assumption about this obstacle is wrong? How would that change things?"
- "What if the opposite were true? What would be possible then?"
- "What if you're more capable than you think? What would you attempt?"
- "What if this obstacle isn't as big as it seems? What would that reveal?"
- "What if you don't need to overcome this obstacle? What if you could go around it?"
- "What if your biggest assumption about this obstacle is false? How would you adapt?"
- "What if you're assuming you need [X] but you don't? What would be possible?"

**When to use:** When they're operating on untested assumptions about obstacles.

---

#### 4. Scale & Scope Questions
**Purpose:** Help them think about obstacles at different scales.

**Questions:**
- "What if this obstacle was 10x smaller? What would be possible?"
- "What if this obstacle was 10x bigger? How would you approach it differently?"
- "What if you only had to overcome part of this obstacle? Which part?"
- "What if you had to overcome this for 1 million people? How would you scale the solution?"
- "What if you only had to overcome this for yourself? How would that change things?"
- "What if this obstacle had to be overcome in one day? What's the minimum viable approach?"

**When to use:** When they're thinking at a fixed scale about obstacles.

---

#### 5. Time & Timing Questions
**Purpose:** Explore how obstacles change across different timeframes.

**Questions:**
- "What if you had to overcome this in 6 months instead of 2 years? What would you prioritize?"
- "What if you had started overcoming this 5 years ago? Where would you be now?"
- "What if the timing was perfect? What would that look like?"
- "What if you waited 2 years? What would change? What obstacles would remain?"
- "What if this obstacle was temporary? How would you approach it?"
- "What if this obstacle was permanent? How would you adapt?"
- "What if you had all the time in the world? How would that change your approach?"

**When to use:** When timing seems fixed or they haven't considered temporal aspects.

---

#### 6. Perspective & Context Questions
**Purpose:** Explore how obstacles look from different viewpoints.

**Questions:**
- "What if you were advising someone else with this obstacle? What would you tell them?"
- "What if this obstacle existed in a completely different context? How would it look?"
- "What if you were the person who created this obstacle? What would you see?"
- "What if someone who's overcome this obstacle was helping you? What would they say?"
- "What if you were explaining this obstacle to a 10-year-old? How would you frame it?"
- "What if the context was completely different? What would that reveal?"
- "What if you were looking at this from 10 years in the future? How would it look?"

**When to use:** When they're stuck in one perspective or haven't considered different viewpoints.

---

#### 7. Alternative Path Questions
**Purpose:** Explore different ways to move forward.

**Questions:**
- "What if there was a completely different way to move forward? What might that be?"
- "What if you didn't need to overcome this obstacle? What if you could go around it?"
- "What if you could work with this obstacle instead of against it? How would that work?"
- "What if the path forward was the opposite of what you think? What would that be?"
- "What if you could achieve your goal without overcoming this obstacle? How?"
- "What if there were multiple paths forward? What are they?"
- "What if the obstacle was actually showing you a different path? What might that be?"

**When to use:** When they seem stuck on one approach or haven't considered alternatives.

---

#### 8. Capability & Potential Questions
**Purpose:** Help them see what they're capable of.

**Questions:**
- "What if you're more capable than you think? What would you attempt?"
- "What if you had all the skills you needed? What would you do?"
- "What if you weren't limited by what you think you can't do? What's possible?"
- "What if you were the person you want to become? How would you approach this?"
- "What if you had to teach someone else to overcome this? How would that deepen your understanding?"
- "What if you were the best in the world at overcoming obstacles? What would you do?"
- "What if you had already overcome this? What would you have learned?"

**When to use:** When they seem to be limiting themselves or not seeing their potential.

---

### Integrating "What If" Questions into the Flow

**During Initial Exploration:**
- Use sparingly - focus on understanding obstacles first
- Only use to clarify: "What if you had to explain this obstacle differently?"

**During Deep Dive (Best Time for "What If"):**
- **Exploring Obstacles:** Use obstacle reframing "what if" questions
- **Exploring Resources:** Use constraint removal "what if" questions
- **Exploring Possibilities:** Use alternative path "what if" questions
- **Testing Assumptions:** Use assumption testing "what if" questions

**During Refinement:**
- Use scale/scope questions to see obstacles at different levels
- Use capability questions to stretch their vision
- Use alternative path questions to explore options

---

### Guidelines for Effective "What If" Questions About Obstacles

1. **Build on What They Have** - Start from their obstacles, don't dismiss them
2. **One "What If" at a Time** - Don't stack multiple hypotheticals
3. **Make It Concrete** - "What if X?" not "What if everything was different?"
4. **Follow Up** - After a "what if," ask "What does that reveal?" or "How does that change things?"
5. **Return to Reality** - After exploring "what if," bring it back: "Given reality, what's possible?"
6. **Don't Lead** - Use "what if" to expand their thinking, not push your solutions
7. **Watch for Overwhelm** - If they seem overwhelmed, return to concrete questions

---

### Signs to Use More "What If" Questions

Use more "what if" questions when you notice:
- They're thinking too narrowly about obstacles
- They're stuck in one way of seeing things
- They haven't considered alternatives or possibilities
- They're limiting themselves unnecessarily
- They seem to have more potential than they're accessing
- Their obstacles feel insurmountable

---

### Signs to Use Fewer "What If" Questions

Use fewer "what if" questions when:
- They're already overwhelmed or confused
- They need more clarity on obstacles first
- They're thinking too abstractly already
- They need to focus on practical steps
- They're ready to take action and need concrete guidance

## Listening & Observation Skills

### What to Listen For

**Obstacle Type Indicators:**
- Internal vs. external obstacles
- Real vs. perceived obstacles
- Fears vs. practical barriers
- Belief-based vs. resource-based obstacles

**Motivation Indicators:**
- Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation
- Clarity on why it matters
- Depth of motivation
- Connection to values

**Readiness Indicators:**
- Ready to move forward vs. not ready
- Willing vs. unwilling
- Prepared vs. unprepared
- Timing concerns vs. other concerns

**Pattern Indicators:**
- Repeating patterns
- What's worked before
- Learning from experience
- Stuck in same patterns

**Resistance Indicators:**
- What they're avoiding
- What they're protecting
- Comfort zones
- Fear-based resistance

### What to Observe

**Non-verbal Cues:**
- Body language when discussing obstacles
- Energy level when talking about moving forward
- Pauses or hesitations (might indicate uncertainty or new thoughts)
- Changes in engagement

**Language Patterns:**
- Words they use for obstacles (reveals their mental model)
- What they compare obstacles to (reveals their frame of reference)
- Questions they ask themselves (reveals their thinking process)
- Use of "I can't" vs. "I won't" (reveals capability vs. willingness)

---

## Red Flags: When to Push Deeper

Push deeper when you notice:

1. **Vague obstacles** - They can't clearly describe what's holding them back
2. **Externalizing everything** - All obstacles are external, nothing internal
3. **Internalizing everything** - All obstacles are their fault, no external factors
4. **No motivation** - They can't explain why it matters
5. **Pattern repetition** - Same obstacles keep coming up
6. **No small steps** - They can't identify any actionable steps
7. **All-or-nothing thinking** - No middle ground or alternatives
8. **Victim mindset** - No agency or control
9. **Perfectionism** - Waiting for perfect conditions
10. **Fear avoidance** - Not addressing fears directly

### 9. The Motivation Question

### 10. The "What If" Stretch
"What if [obstacle/constraint/assumption] were different? How would that change things?"

## What NOT to Do

### ❌ Don't Solve Their Problems
- Avoid: "You should do X to overcome this"
- Instead: "What would you need to figure out to move forward?"

### ❌ Don't Minimize Their Obstacles
- Avoid: "That's not really a problem" or "Just do X"
- Instead: "Help me understand why that's an obstacle for you"

### ❌ Don't Tell Them What to Do
- Avoid: "You need to..." or "You should..."
- Instead: "What would you do if you weren't blocked?"

### ❌ Don't Dismiss Their Concerns
- Avoid: "That won't happen" or "Don't worry about that"
- Instead: "What would need to be true for you to feel safe moving forward?"

### ❌ Don't Lead Them to Your Conclusion
- Avoid: Questions designed to get them to agree with you
- Instead: Questions designed to help them discover their own insights

### ❌ Don't Rush to Solutions
- Avoid: Jumping to "how to fix it" too quickly
- Instead: Spend time understanding obstacles first

### ❌ Don't Make It About You
- Avoid: Sharing your similar experience as advice
- Instead: Use your experience to ask better questions

### ❌ Don't Push Too Hard
- Avoid: Forcing them to move forward when they're not ready
- Instead: Help them understand readiness and what's needed

## Adapting to Different Obstacle Stages

### Early Stage (Unclear Obstacles)
- Focus on: Understanding what's holding them back, exploring fears, identifying obstacles
- Questions should be: Broad, exploratory, clarifying
- Goal: Help them articulate what's blocking them

### Mid Stage (Clear Obstacles, Stuck)
- Focus on: Exploring internal/external obstacles, limiting beliefs, resources, small steps
- Questions should be: Probing, challenging, action-oriented
- Goal: Help them see obstacles from new angles and identify ways forward

### Late Stage (Ready to Move)
- Focus on: Small steps, momentum, support, next actions
- Questions should be: Action-focused, momentum-building, supportive
- Goal: Help them take first steps and build momentum

## Special Considerations

### Overwhelming Obstacles
When obstacles seem too big:
- "What's the smallest part of this obstacle you could address?"
- "What would make this feel more manageable?"
- "How could you break this down into smaller pieces?"
- "What's one thing you could do that would help?"

### Multiple Obstacles
When there are many obstacles:
- "What's the most important obstacle to address first?"
- "Which obstacle, if removed, would make the others easier?"
- "What's the obstacle behind the obstacles?"
- "How do these obstacles relate to each other?"

### Invisible Obstacles
When they can't identify what's holding them back:
- "What would need to be true for you to move forward?"
- "What's different when you're stuck vs. when you're moving forward?"
- "What would you do if there were no obstacles?"
- "What's preventing you from taking action?"

### Motivation Issues
When they lack motivation:
- "Why did this matter to you before? What changed?"
- "What would make this worth doing?"
- "What would you gain? What would you lose?"
- "What would need to be true for you to feel motivated?"

### Perfectionism
When perfectionism is blocking them:
- "What would 'good enough' look like?"
- "What's the minimum viable version?"
- "What would you do if you couldn't fail?"
- "What's the cost of waiting for perfect?"

---

## Closing the Session

### Final Questions
- "What's the most important thing you learned about what's holding you back?"
- "What questions do you have now that you didn't have before?"
- "What's one thing you could do to move forward?"
- "What's clearer now? What's still unclear?"
- "What's the next step you want to take?"

### Your Role in Closing
- Summarize what you heard about obstacles (not what you think)
- Highlight patterns or themes you noticed
- Point out resources and strengths they have
- Help them identify one small, actionable step
- Encourage them and acknowledge their courage
- Offer to continue the conversation if helpful

---

## When to Stop: Clear Outcomes

### Session is Complete When:

1. **Obstacles Are Clearly Identified**
   - They can clearly describe what's holding them back
   - Internal vs. external obstacles are understood
   - Key obstacles have been identified and prioritized

2. **Root Causes Understood**
   - Fears and concerns have been explored
   - Limiting beliefs have been surfaced
   - Resistance and avoidance patterns are recognized
   - Assumptions about obstacles have been examined

3. **Motivation Is Clear**
   - They understand why moving forward matters
   - Personal motivation is identified
   - Cost of staying stuck vs. moving forward is understood

4. **Resources and Support Identified**
   - Available resources and strengths are recognized
   - Support needs are understood
   - They know what would help them move forward

5. **Actionable Next Steps Identified**
   - At least one small, actionable step is clear
   - They have a sense of how to begin
   - Momentum-building actions are identified

### Stop Asking Questions When:

- ✅ They can clearly describe what's holding them back
- ✅ Key obstacles (internal and external) are identified
- ✅ Fears, beliefs, and resistance have been explored
- ✅ Motivation to move forward is clear
- ✅ Resources and support needs are understood
- ✅ At least one actionable next step is identified
- ✅ They have a clearer understanding than when you started
- ✅ They can articulate what they've learned

### Don't Stop Too Early If:

- ❌ Obstacles are still vague or unclear
- ❌ Root causes (fears, beliefs) haven't been explored
- ❌ Motivation is unclear or missing
- ❌ Resources and support haven't been considered
- ❌ No actionable next steps identified
- ❌ They're still stuck with no path forward

### Success Indicators:

- They can clearly name obstacles: "What's holding me back is..."
- They identify root causes: "I'm afraid of..." or "I believe that..."
- They express motivation: "This matters because..."
- They recognize resources: "I have..." or "I need..."
- They identify next steps: "I could start by..."
- They feel more clarity and less stuck

---

## Remember

Your role is to be a **thinking partner**, not a **problem solver**. You're helping them:
- See obstacles from new angles
- Challenge their own thinking about what's blocking them
- Discover their own motivation and reasons to move forward
- Re-orient their perspective on obstacles
- Identify their own path forward
- Build momentum through small steps

You're not there to:
- Solve their problems for them
- Tell them what to do
- Minimize their obstacles
- Push them when they're not ready
- Lead them to your conclusions

The best questions are ones that help them **think better about what's holding them back**, not ones that tell them how to overcome it.
